We Intervene

Client Project

User Flow | Information Architecture | Prototyping

Overview

Product Type:

Responsive Web Application

Role:

UX Designer

Duration:

3 Weeks

Tools:

Concepts (iOS), Figma, FigJam, Trello, Jira, Google Drive, Google Forms, Calendly, Maze, UserTesting.com, Zoom

We Intervene is an education-technology startup that empowers student support staff to make data-driven decisions to enhance student success. These staff members often support large numbers of students and parents simultaneously.

My team and I were assigned to create a parent portal that facilitated the management of resources, referrals, and communication between support staff and parents. My primary responsibilities in this project were centered on user flows, wireframing, and prototyping.

The project was completed during a 2-week design sprint, resulting in the delivery of a high-fidelity, interactive prototype.

Problem

Parents need a convenient and straightforward means of accessing resources and communications from their children's schools, as they are often bombarded with information and have other responsibilities, such as work and parenting commitments.

Solution

Simplifying User Flows

Simplified the user flows for how parents searched, accessed, and saved resources they were interested in.

Reducing Information Overload

Reduce the amount of unnecessary information and confusing terminology on the parent-side dashboard.

Phase 1: Discover

Methodologies:

User Surveys, User Interviews, Competitive & Comparative Analysis, Feature Inventory, Task Analysis

Research

Our initial research revealed that users frequently found their experiences with student success platforms to be counterintuitive and not very helpful. To gain a deeper understanding of this, we conducted user interviews with parents from a school in Brooklyn, NY.

6 Parent Interviews

5 of 6 Students Were High Schoolers

Our user interviews showed that parents had 5 main complaints:

Ease of Access

Parents struggled with figuring out how to actually sign in and access the information on the student success platforms.

Notifications

Parents want the ability to choose how they receive communications and resources from the school.

Information Overload

Schools end up sending too many resources, some of them not even applicable to the parents’ or students’ needs.

Waste Time

Parents end up having to spend more time sifting through what was sent home or finding more relevant resources on their own.

Communication

Parents found it difficult to communicate with the school and student support staff.

Competitive & Comparative Analysis

In order to understand what other platforms were offering and where we can add value, a competitive and comparative analysis was done through a feature inventory.

We Intervene C&C Analysis

Our analysis revealed that a lack of communication and difficulties accessing parent dashboards were major sources of frustration for users. Implementing a more streamlined communication system between parents and school staff would result in fewer confusion and less wasted time for parents.

Phase 2: Define

Methodologies:

Problem & How Might We Statements, User Persona, User Flow, Site Map

Ideation

With the user pain points in mind, we developed How Might We statements asking ourselves:

“How might we provide a way for users to access communications and resources from schools in an organized and prioritized, mobile-friendly format?”

User Flow

In order to understand how users communicate with school support staff and access resources, a user flow was created to identify potential areas for improvement.

There were 2 main tasks that I wanted to make sure that users could accomplish without any errors.

Find, Save, and Sync Student Event to Calendar

Send and Receive Messages to and from Student Support Staff

Phase 3: Design

Methodologies:

Sketching, Low-Fidelity - High-Fidelity Wireframing, Design Systems, Prototyping

Lo-Fi Wireframes

Using the user flows as a guide, I was able to plan out how the different screens should look and work together.

1. Student Status Card

Provides parents with general information about their student(s) at a quick glance.

2. New Resources

Newly added resources for parents. Moved towards the top for easier visibility.

3. New Messages

New messages from student support staff. Moved towards the top for easier visibility.

Phase 4: Deliver

Methodologies:

Usability Testing (Moderated and Unmoderated), A/B Testing, Iterations

Usability Testing

To ensure that users could intuitively navigate and complete the main user flows, my team and I created 2 tasks with a set of 4 metrics.

Task: 1

You’re interested in learning about colleges. Locate a College Fair resources and add it to your calendar.

Task: 2

You received an email about volunteering at the YMCA. Open and save the resource to your favorites.

5 Total User Tests

5 of 5 Users Completed Both Tasks

5 of 5 Users Found the Onboarding Useful

4 of 5 Users Found Wording Confusing

Iteration

With the usability test results, our team was able to implement necessary changes to our designs where we noticed user hesitation.

We found that some of the language and icons were unfamiliar and confusing for users, so we conducted A/B Testing to determine the best options.

Hi-Fi Prototype

After finalizing the icons and language, the Hi-Fi Prototype was complete!

But Wait, There's More

During the research phase, my team noticed the trend that users often times wanted the option to view their student success management software via mobile whether it was because they were already constantly on their phones for work, or just out of convenience.

Fortunately, we were able to meet our scope requirements so we created a minimum viable product (MVP) for the mobile version.

Since We Intervene’s platform is a web application it can be developed responsively so it would not require a separate application.

Mobile Wireframe

Reflection

Impact

After presenting our research and design to our client, the look on her face said it all. She was very pleased with our prototype with the parent portal and said the she agrees with the importance of the chat translation feature. She also mentioned that the user onboarding would be very effective at reducing the learning curve involved for new users.

We Intervene is looking to launch this product later this year (2021), partnering with a local high school in Queens, New York.

Challenges

During this 3 week sprint, my team and I faced many different challenges. For the most part it was inevitable simply due to the fact that the target user are working parents. They have busy lives and things come up, which ultimately is out of our control. Nonetheless, we faced these challenges head on and in the end, I am very proud of my team as we delivered a successful and well-polished final product.
